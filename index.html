Anne Brontë’s Poetry of Childhood: A Digital Scholarly Edition

edited by Ciara Glasscott

An edition submitted in partial fulfilment of the Degree of Doctor of Philosophy in the Discipline of English, School of English, Media and Creative Arts, College of Arts, Social Sciences & Celtic Studies, University of Galway. September 2024.

The time is ripe to bring more public and critical attention to Anne Brontë's poetry. This scholarly digital edition provides access to newly edited digital texts of ten of Brontë's poems. Manuscript images of these poems are brought together from a range of international institutions, providing direct access to Brontë's work. Brontë's poetry is historically overlooked within her oeuvre. Brontë herself was traditionally positioned as the 'third' Brontë, with her work being deemed less worthy of critical attention. Much work has been done to shift this focus in recent decades, building up from the feminist reclamation of her much maligned second novel The Tenant of Wildfell Hall of the 1980s to a purposeful reframing of Anne Brontë's status as an important Victorian novelist in her own right in the 21st century.

Despite these changes to Brontë's reputation, the lack of rigorous scholarly research centring on her poetry stands out as a glaring omission, with the troubling tendency to focus on autobiographical readings undermining readings of Brontë's poetry as a crucial component of her literary project. This trend is in keeping with critical dismissals of women's lyric poetry as sentimental. Anne Brontë's poetry crosses genres, with much of her early work taking place in the fantasy world of Gondal that she created with her sister Emily as children. Even as Anne developed other modes of poetry, she continued to regularly write Gondal poems, using it as a vehicle from which to explore complicated social and emotional issues in innovative and insightful ways. This edition refocuses attention on Anne Brontë as a poet by providing access to and commentary on her manuscripts.

This site presents two separate editions of these poems. One is a documentary edition in which full diplomatic transcriptions of the poems are presented alongside the corresponding manuscript images, keeping focus on the texts of the manuscript versions. The critical edition of the poems presents a text which maintains the editorial preference for following the manuscript, but with an interface which provdes users with the option to examine the variations made by Brontë in later published versions. The critical edition provides additional context about the publishing history of each poem and critical commentary elucidating Brontë’s creative process at work, highlighting and analysing changes to the manuscript or between the manuscript and the later versions published by Brontë. This combined editorial method reveals intriguing new insights into the development of Brontë's philosophical and literary approach.

This edition is part of my PhD project Representations of childhood in the work of Anne Brontë. The thesis is a close study of Brontë's engagement with the concept of childhood across her oeuvre, paying particular attention to her positioning on questions of pedagogical practice and Romanticism. This edition focuses on Brontë's poems involving childhood. This focus results in a spread of poems from early to more mature work and includes both Gondal and non-Gondal poetry. The changes made within the manuscripts and between versions contribute further, by highlighting easily overlooked features of Brontë's positioning on the topics of childhood, motherhood and Romanticism. The framing of childhood is also a central question within both of her novels, Agnes Grey and The Tenant of Wildfell Hall as both focus on education as a means of enacting societal reform. By introducing Anne Brontë’s understudied poetry into this important and complicated dialogue, the production of this edition adds valuable context to our understanding of her literary project.
