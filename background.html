Background

Biography

Anne Brontë was born in Bradford in 1820 as the youngest of six children to Patrick and Maria Brontë. Along with her surviving sisters Charlotte and Emily, Anne became an author of fiction and poetry.

After the early death of her mother, Anne was close to her maternal Aunt Branwell, from whom she developed a deep religious ethic and her own deep personal belief in universal salvation. Unlike her older sisters, Anne was too young to attend the infamous Cowan Bridge school where her eldest two sisters both became terminally ill and was educated at home in Haworth until attending boarding school in her teens.

As young children Anne and her sister Emily created an imaginary world called Gondal. This fantasy setting influences much of Brontë’s poetry which was often based upon these creative heightened scenarios.

Anne worked as a governess, most significantly with the Robinson family for five years. She only left the Robinsons after her brother Branwell (who was working as a tutor for the family on Anne’s recommendation) caused a scandal by having an affair with the married Lady Robinson. Nevertheless, Anne remained friends with her students throughout the remainder of her life.

After returning to Haworth, Anne worked with her sisters Charlotte and Emily on shared goals of literary publishing. The three sisters self-funded the publication of a volume of poetry in 1846. Following this, Emily and Anne’s first novels, Agnes Grey and Wuthering Heights, were accepted for publication, eventually being published in Autumn 1847. Anne then followed up with The Tenant of Wildfell Hall in 1848.

Anne Brontë's fiction is characterised by a dauntless confrontation of societal abuses and met with a mixed reception, particularly The Tenant of Wildfell Hall's startlingly modern portrayal of coercive control in a marriage. Anne was stalwart in her defence of her novel, penning a fiery preface to its second edition, and travelling to London to boldly declare her identity after confusion over the sisters’ pseudonyms caused conflicts between their different publishers. With the growth of her critical reputation, this unabashed conviction has since been recognised as her greatest strength, pushing back on her traditional cultural image as the quiet, shy Brontë sister.

After tragedy struck the family with the successive shocking premature deaths of Branwell and Emily, Anne’s health deteriorated, and she ultimately passed away in June 1849 at the age of 29. She persuaded Charlotte to bring her to Scarborough in her last weeks, with a final hope in the curative powers of the sea air expressed in her letter to their friend Ellen Nussey who accompanied them (Smith 544-45). She is the only Brontë not to be buried in the Haworth cemetery as befits her outward looking gaze.

Publishing history of Anne Brontë’s poetry

A selection of sixteen of Anne Brontë's poems were included by the sisters in their joint poetry collection Poems by Currer, Ellis and Acton Bell which was published by Aylott and Jones with self-financing by the sisters in 1846. The four of Anne's Gondal poems which were included in this edition ("A Fragment", "Parting address from Z.Z to A.E", "The Consolation" & "The Student’s Serenade") had their Gondal signatures removed, along with some updating of language and phrasing. The suppression of the fantastical elements of the Gondal poems serves to give her contribution a more uniform presentation as a set of lyric poems, many of which mediate on themes of faith. Two of Anne's poems, "The Narrow Way" and "The Three Guides" were published by Fraser's Magazine in 1848, the only two Brontë poems to be published independently during their lifetimes.

After Anne's death Charlotte published another ten of her sister's poems as part of the Selections from the Literary Remains of Ellis and Acton Bell, in some cases with significant changes. Most notably, her final poem was published as "Last Lines" in a shortened and rearranged version of the original manuscript text. The rest were published by various other editors in the later nineteenth and early twentieth century before being brought together in a complete collection in Edward Chitham's 1979 edition of The Poems of Anne Brontë (updated and republished in 2021). Within Chitham's edition the text of the poems is "derived from a series of fair-copy MSS" (192). Chitham follows the manuscripts on titles and "indentation and line division" while "[s]pelling is modernised with manuscript divergence given in footnotes" (xix).
