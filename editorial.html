Editorial introduction

Introduction

Anne Brontë's poetry comprises a body of approximately fifty surviving poems with a broad range of topics, genre and forms. Her Gondal project begins as fantasy juvenilia written with her sister Emily and this imaginative escapism is adapted as a vehicle to explore more complex existential matters in later Gondal poems. Brontë's non-Gondal poems include dialogue poems that engage in increasingly nuanced intellectual and aesthetic debates, as well as meditations on faith, family, and loneliness. Yet this rich oeuvre has rarely been the topic of serious critical attention and gaining access to the poems themselves can prove challenging. A selection of Brontë's poems were published in the compilation volume Poems by Currer, Ellis and Acton Bell which was written and submitted by all three Brontë sisters and published by Aylott & Jones in 1846. However, the remainder of Brontë’s poems were still unpublished before her premature death in 1849, meaning that readers have had to rely on other editorial mediations when reading many of her poems. Moreover, there are many interesting facets of Brontë's authorial process and intellectual positioning which can be illuminated by a close focus on the manuscripts. Therefore, a central element of my PhD project has been to create a digital scholarly edition of a selection of Anne Brontë's poetry.

Within my thesis, my focus is on Brontë's handling of themes of childhood, pedagogy and Romanticism across her work. When it comes to her poetry, there are ten poems which speak to these themes most productively and form the backbone of my argument. I selected these ten poems for my edition to provide thematic cohesion for the edition and editorial insights for the thesis. Encountering a poem in a manuscript creates an entirely different sense of the nature and personality of the poem. This more unfiltered sense of the poet's process and the deliberate crafting of the poem can often be enhanced with alterations being made to the poem in the manuscripts which are not conveyed as forcefully to the reader by a footnote in a printed text. Therefore, providing the user with access to the digital facsimiles of the manuscript materials is a central element of my edition. While this can be achieved through a print edition with facing pages of the manuscript and a transcription, a digital edition offers the possibility to visualise facsimile images of the manuscript and to view different versions of the poem which have appeared in print. The decision to make the edition a digital one was therefore borne out of the desire to create an easily accessible, reliable, and informative edition of the texts which provides the user with the opportunity to view the manuscript directly alongside a layer of editorial analysis of these poems and their textual history. Until the release of the second edition of Edward Chitham's carefully complied The Poems of Anne Brontë in early 2021 (coinciding with Brontë's bicentenary celebrations), there had been no easy way to access a reliable scholarly edition of Brontë's poems - Chitham's original 1979 edition had gone out of print, meaning modern readers would have been reliant on historical reprints of older, and potentially unverified versions of her work if they had an interest in reading or studying the poetry. Therefore, these barriers to accessing Anne Brontë's poetry, particularly in comparison to other Brontë texts, reinforces the habitual minimisation of Brontë as a poet. The recent republication of Chitham's text combined with the uptick in popular and scholarly interest in Anne Brontë in recent years indicate an increased appetite for this aspect of Brontë's work, making a digital edition of Brontë's poems rather timely. Moreover, the edition will be freely available to all, providing immediate access to a unique selection of Brontë's poems which are physically separated by the Atlantic Ocean.

I decided to create both a documentary and a critical edition of the selected poems that the viewer can freely move between. The documentary edition provides a diplomatic transcription of the text of each poem as found in the manuscript. This transcription then forms the base text from which I built the text of the critical edition. For the critical edition I provide the variations found in the poems which were published in the 1846 Poems by Currer, Ellis and Acton Bell, as well as providing editorial context and commentary which help to elucidate the poems and their textual states for users. The combination of both editions gives users the opportunity to read the more stripped back documentary edition with its focus on the manuscript and to go to the critical edition for more information on the poem's textual history and editorial analysis. All of these elements, and the editorial processes which enacted them, provided crucial support to my understanding of Brontë's intellectual and philosophical positioning within her work.

Background

Digital editions are considered "a key interest in the Digital Humanities" (Franzini et al. 2), with the field encompassing a wide range of divergent approaches to textual editing. An umbrella definition of digital textual editions as "projects that make use of digital technologies to reproduce editions and transcriptions of literary texts, be those inscribed on tablets or penned on papyrus, vellum, or paper" (2) is a useful starting point. Franzini et al. also describe some of the key distinguishing features of different types of digital textual editions:

[s]cholars distinguish between digital and digitised editions, the former indicating interactivity as opposed to mere reproductions of analogue 
material, and between digital editions and digital scholarly editions, the latter describing a project with a strong critical component 
(Franzini et.al 2).

My edition is digital as it is not simply a digitised version of an existing text but rather comprises a discrete and unique digital text. Moreover, the edition is interactive, providing the user with the full-texts of the selected versions of each poem, allowing them to navigate between different views of each poem. The interactive aspect of the edition further confirms that the edition is digital rather than digitised. The question of a digital edition versus a digital scholarly edition is a little more complex and gets to the heart of the editorial process being followed here.

This edition is most interested in the manuscript version of Brontë's texts and the poems chosen here only exist in one manuscript version, meaning that options for editorial approaches are automatically narrowed. When choosing between a documentary edition or a more curated approach, there are some interesting arguments to consider. Elena Pierazzo notes that the documentary edition which "puts documents at the heart of the editorial care" (74) is generally considered more relevant when dealing with one witness (76) but that there has also been some snobbery around this approach meaning that, in practice, "there is a sort of critical edition [which] has traditionally still been provided even in texts surviving in a single witness, with the provision of a clear text, with internal variations and apparent errors set aside" (76-7). The latter is a very accurate description of Chitham's approach, which focuses on providing a clear reading text of the manuscript, albeit with some editorial emendations. This choice partly stems from the conventions of the print form; it would probably be rather startling to many readers to pick up a book of Victorian poetry and find some poems in tiny unpunctuated blocks, not to mention decidedly challenging to read.

The digital nature of this edition also comes into play when considering the usability of a documentary edition. A documentary edition may not be the preferred choice for a print edition as irregularities in presentation may be jarring to the reader in that conventional context. By contrast, the digital medium provides many more options in terms of form. Therefore, the potential to view an image of the manuscript alongside a transcription gives the user a new relationship to the form in which the text was first instantiated. This encourages the user to view and engage with the physical attributes of the original MS version of the text alongside providing an easily readable transcription of the poem with some extra-textual history and other editorial materials for those who are interested. Pierazzo suggests that it is this ability to get close to the MS in a readable way which contributes to the rehabilitation of the documentary edition in the context of the rising popularity of digital scholarly editing (75), saying that "text encoding has in fact enabled editors to have their cake and eat it: features that were once normalized without mercy to produce reading, critical (or syntagmatic) editions, can now be retained and simply switched on and off at the leisure to please different types of audiences" (81). Pierazzo also notes that some users, encountering the digital documentary edition of Jane Austen's work, were "shocked" (77) because they were "not used to being exposed to the messiness of the authorial work" (77), particularly in an author they expected to be very well-organised and clean. This shock, in itself, can be valuable, as it means that the user is being put in a position to reconfigure their preconceptions about the author and/or the text due to their exposure to the less polished texts that are common features of authors’ drafting and writing processes.

This edition as a whole is a digital scholarly edition with the critical version featuring additional components which are intended to bring the user’s experience into a broader critical realm than the documentary option. Peter Robinson notes the tension between encoding the text primarily as 'text-as-work' versus 'text-as document' in the emerging debates about digital editions, suggesting that many documentary style digital editions that prioritise 'text-as-document' offer little to no way into the edition except on a page by page basis thus excluding "the possibility of recording text-as-work" (126). Robinson further sees this method as one that risks "distanc[ing] ourselves and our editions from the readers" (127) and contends that it is also worthwhile to make the most of the fact that the "digital medium is perfectly adapted to enactment of editions as an ever-continuing negotiation between editors, readers, documents, texts and works" (127), making the case for expansive digital scholarly editions. The first choice which brings the edition in this direction is the inclusion of the texts which appear in the 1846 Poems which the user can easily swap between or compare. Whereas later publications have been edited by other hands, Brontë's alterations to the 1846 versions of the text have more to insights to offer on Brontë's philosophical and aesthetic choices. Furthermore, there is an additional layer of editorial annotations for the user to engage with, including in text notes and individual editor’s notes featuring textual history and analytical material.

The edition consists of a selection of 10 of Brontë's poems. At a later date this project might be expanded to include the full 52 poems by Brontë; however, this scope was appropriate for the current project and the limited number gives the opportunity for all relevant versions of the poems to be included in full, with space for detailed editorial annotation and analysis. The poems were chosen based on their relevance to my written thesis's focus on the representation of childhood and pedagogy in Brontë's oeuvre. This gives the selection of poems in the edition a thematic coherence so that the user can see connections and development between the texts in terms of features such as Romantic imagery, parent-child relationships and nostalgia. Moreover, this focus allows the textual editing work to directly impact on my thesis work, as manuscript information comes to light which casts more light on Brontë's approach to these ideas.

Creating the edition

When considering the best methodological approach to take when constructing the edition the Text Encoding Initiative (TEI) was one of the primary options to investigate. The TEI defines itself as:

a consortium which collectively develops and maintains a standard for the representation of texts in digital form. Its chief deliverable is 
a set of Guidelines which specify encoding methods for machine-readable texts, chiefly in the humanities, social sciences and linguistics 
(TEI "Home").

Moreover, the TEI is among the most frequently used encoding standards for humanities texts - in Franzini et al.'s study, 56% of digital editions considered used TEI-based XML files (16) and Georg Rhem describes it as "[o]ne of the most widely used annotation systems in Linguistics and Digital Humanities" (301). Using a well-established academic standard ensures high levels of interoperability and reusability, as well as compatibility with a wide range of visualisation software options. This choice is further supported by the idea that "[e]ditors especially advocate this practice as a suitable means of marking up and publishing texts online" (Franzini et al. 16). Moreover, Robinson notes that "the Text Encoding Initiative guidelines have long offered comprehensive means of encoding text-as-work… as well as the recently developed system for encoding text-as-document" (124) meaning that the software is well-suited to producing both the documentary and critical transcriptions of the poems. In order to encode the poems using the TEI encoding language I decided to use Oxygen XML Editor.

To visualise the edition, I chose to use EVT (Edition Visualisation Technology) software. EVT is built for the specific purpose of visualising and publishing TEI-encoded digital scholarly editions, making it a perfect fit for this project. Oxygen has developed default methods of visualising variation between different witnesses, making it straightforward for the user to see and investigate changes between the versions. Moreover, it enables visualisation of deletions and additions within the manuscript, and its customisability allowed for solutions to more complex deletions that span several lines, such as the pencil 'X' in the final stanza of "Dreams". All of these features of EVT helped to contribute to the production of the edition as a usable and informative resource which remains centred on the manuscript while providing different extra-textual information and analysis.

Sources used

When creating the edition, I used Poems by Currer, Ellis and Acton Bell (1846) as the basis for encoding the editorial variations of this witness within the critical version of the edition. The primary sources I've used are the original Anne Brontë manuscripts. The ten poems are spread across six different manuscripts which are held by three institutions: the Brontë Parsonage Museum, the Morgan Library and Museum and the Berg Collection at the New York Public Library. Permission has kindly been granted to reproduce the images in this edition and the institutions retain copyright over the images of the manuscripts.
