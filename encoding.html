Encoding Methodology

Introduction

This digital scholarly edition of a selection of Anne Brontë's poetry has been produced alongside my written thesis on Brontë’s work as part of my PhD project. Anne Brontë's intriguing and layered body of poetry occupies a liminal space within the Brontë family oeuvre, being traditionally overlooked in terms of critical analysis. This edition aims to redress this gap by presenting a manuscript-centred edition accompanied by editorial analysis.

The following document outlines the methodology used during the creation of this digital scholarly edition of a selection of Brontë's poems. Step by step details about the encoding methodology and process are provided here to document the process of representing and implementing editorial decisions in a digital medium.

This edition features ten poems with themes relating to my thesis topic of childhood. The poems are presented in two separate editions. One is a documentary edition in which full diplomatic transcriptions of the poems are presented alongside the corresponding manuscript images, keeping focus on the texts of the manuscript versions. The critical edition of the poems presents a text which maintains the editorial preference for following the manuscript, but with an interface which provdes users with the option to examine the variations made by Brontë in later published versions.

The edition was prepared using the Oxygen XML Editor. The critical and documentary editions were prepared as separate XML files that adhere to the Text Encoding Initiative Guidelines. This standards-based method ensure platform-independent interoperability of the files and provides flexibility to produce a functional interactive edition that is inviting and user-friendly.

Definitions of different elements from the official TEI Guidelines have been included where appropriate throughout the document. I have provided links to the relevant sections of the TEI Guidelines, where appropriate.

Developing a consistent approach to the encoding of the edition was complex at times. There were certain fixed technical requirements, however, at times more creativity was needed to arrive at decisions which were both TEI compliant and achieved the desired visual effect when the edition is rendered online via Edition Visualization Technology (EVT). All of these decisions and methods are outlined here in a step by step guide to the editorial principles and construction of the edition. EVT is "a light-weight, open source tool specifically designed to create digital editions from TEI XML-encoded texts, freeing the scholar from the burden of web programming and enabling the final user to browse, explore and study digital editions by means of a user-friendly interface" (Turco et al).

Encoding process

TEI Header

In order to be compliant with the TEI Guidelines it is necessary to begin all documents with the element <teiHeader>, which must contain certain crucial information about the document, its text/s and encoding. While there are a number of possible elements which can be contained within <teiHeader>, the element <fileDesc> is the only one that is mandatory for all documents. [TEI Guidelines 2.2].

File description

The <fileDesc> element "contains a full bibliographic description of an electronic file" [2.2]. Within this element it is also mandatory to include the following elements : <titleStmt>, <publicationStmt> and <sourceDesc> [2.1].

Title statement

The element <titleStmt> "groups information about the title of a work and those responsible for its content" [2.2.1]. This must include the title of the file and usually also includes information on the authors and/or editors of the file as well as any other parties who are responsible for the production of the document. 

<fileDesc>
    <titleStmt>
        <title>Anne Brontë’s Poetry of Childhood: A Digital Scholarly Edition</title>
    </titleStmt>
</fileDesc>
Publication statement

The purpose of <publicationStmt> is “to name the agency by which a resource is made available (for example, a publisher or distributor) and to supply any additional information about the way in which it is made available such as licensing conditions, identifying numbers, etc.” [2.2.4]. 

<publicationStmt>
    <p>Publication Information</p>
    <publisher>University of Galway</publisher>
    <pubPlace>Galway, Ireland</pubPlace>
    <date>2024</date>
    <availability>
        <p>This is an open access work licensed under a Creative Commons Attribution 4.0
            International license.</p>
    </availability>
</publicationStmt>
Source description

The <sourceDesc> element "describes the source(s) from which an electronic text was derived or generated, typically a bibliographic description in the case of a digitized text" [2.2.7].

In the case of this edition, this primarily means including information about the textual witnesses, which include printed and manuscript documents. The <sourceDesc> element spans the breadth of bibliographic information of all witness and contains a variety of elements:

Structured bibliographic citation

The first element included within the <sourceDesc> is <biblStruct> which "contains a structured bibliographic citation, in which only bibliographic sub-elements appear and in a specified order" [3.11.1].

One examples is as follows:

<biblStruct>
    <monogr>
        <author>Anne Brontë</author>
        <title>"Tis Strange to Think"</title>
        <imprint>
            <date>1846</date>
        </imprint>
    </monogr>
</biblStruct>
Witnesses

As this is a digital critical edition, there are a number of different witnesses being documented and encoded. These witnesses are the original Anne Brontë manuscripts and any printed versions of the poems which appeared within Brontë's lifetime.

The information for the witnesses which are used in each poem is included in the <listWit> element which "lists definitions for all the witnesses referred to by a critical apparatus, optionally grouped hierarchically" [12.1.4.2.] <listWit> is included in the <sourceDesc> section of the <teiHeader>.

Each witness within <listWit> is identified by a <wit> element.

For example:

<witness xml:id="MS-Q">
    <listWit>
        <bibl type="manuscript">
            <title>Anne Brontë Manuscript 1842-5</title>
            <msIdentifier>
                <settlement>New York</settlement>
                <repository>Morgan Library and Musuem </repository>
                <idno>MA 28</idno>
            </msIdentifier>
        </bibl>
    </listWit>
</witness>
Witness id elements

Each witness needs to be encoded with a unique @xml:id identifier so that it can be linked to variations within the text. For the printed texts the sigla used were a form of initials for the text e.g. for the 1846 Poems by Currer, Ellis and Acton Bell:

<witness xml:id="ACE">

For the manuscripts I decided to adopt the sigla used by Edward Chitham in his 1979 comprehensive print edition of The Poems of Anne Brontë (updated and reissued in 2021) to facilitate easy cross-referencing of the manuscripts by readers. For example:

<witness xml:id="MS-P">

This id was then used when encoding variations to indicate which readings can be found in each witness:

<rdg wit="#ACE">,</rdg>
Witness information

When providing information about the text within the <wit> element, I used the <bibl> element to group relevant bibliographic information about the witnesses.

For manuscripts, this includes the manuscript title, with information about the repository where the manuscript is housed and its catalogue number included within the <msIdentifier> element:

<bibl type="manuscript">
    <title>Anne Brontë Manuscript, 1838-41</title>
    <msIdentifier>
        <settlement>New York</settlement>
        <repository>Morgan Library and Museum</repository>
        <idno>MA 2696.5</idno>
    </msIdentifier>
</bibl>

The information included in the <bibl> element for published books provides basic bibliographic information on the text:

<bibl type="book">
    <title>Poems by Currer, Ellis, and Acton Bell</title>
    <author>Charlotte Brontë</author>
    <author>Emily Brontë</author>
    <author>Anne Brontë</author>
    <date>1846</date>
    <publisher>Aylott and Jones</publisher>
</bibl>
Texts

Each poem was initially encoded in its own TEI XML document to allow for ease of testing features in Edition Visualisation Technology (EVT) software. These individual files were them combined into one complete file for the documentary version and a second for the critical version of the edition.

Each poem in the combined file is encoded in a separate <text> element. In order to link the poem to images of the manuscripts they need to be identified as separate texts within the final document. Therefore, each poem’s title is recorded with the @n attribute and given a unique @xml:id which is a contraction of the poem’s name. This information is recorded within the <text> element to ensure that the entire poem is correctly recorded as a discrete text:

<text n="Tis Strange to think" xml:id="TIS">
Front matter and titles

After the <text> element the encoding of the poem itself can begin. The first aspect of the poem to consider is the title of the poem. The preferred method of encoding paratextual information such as the title or information on a title page is by tagging it with the <front> element which "contains any prefatory matter (headers, abstracts, title page, prefaces, dedications, etc.) found at the start of a document, before the main body" [4.6].

Within the <front> element the correct choice for encoding the poem’s title is the <head> element which "contains any type of heading, for example the title of a section, or the heading of a list, glossary, manuscript description, etc." [4.2.1].

An example would look as follows:

<front>
    <head>Tis Strange to Think</head>
</front>
Text body

After the completion of the <front> element, the text of the poem is encoded within a <body> element. The text body element “contains the whole body of a single unitary text, excluding any front or back matter” [4].

Manuscript images

The edition features parallel images of the manuscripts alongside transcriptions of the text. Therefore, images of the manuscript need to be linked to the texts so that EVT will recognise which images to display.

The first step for this process is to give each image a unique filename using an @xml:id attribute. This way, once the image with the corresponding filename is placed in the relevant data folder of EVT (\data\images\single), the software will recognise the link and display the appropriate image. The @xml:id attribute is placed within a <pb/> element, which indicates a page break. This allows the images and the text to become fully linked so that turning the virtual page of the manuscript will lead to the appropriate portion of transcribed text appearing on screen. Therefore, at every change of image a new numbered <pb> element indicates which image should be displayed.

For example, the first instance of page-image alignment in a document might be encoded as follows:

<pb n="1" xml:id="TIS_1" facs="data/images/single/TIS_1.jpg/>

With "TIS_1" referring to the image file "TIS_1.jpg" housed in the relevant EVT data folder.

A subsequent change of image would then look like this:

<pb n="2" xml:id="TIS_2" facs="data/images/single/TIS_2.jpg/>
Textual features
Text structure

When encoding the poems, there are some benefits to signifying changes in stanzas. Both <lg> and <div> would work in this capacity, however EVT’s default configuration did not recognise <lg> as a discrete stanza so I decided to use <div> as follows:

<div type="stanza">

This allows the poem to render correctly in EVT without additional customisation and clearly demarcates between different stanzas while using valid TEI markup.

I decided to label and number each individual line of poetry within each text. This makes it straightforward to find and refer to verse lines and is the preferred practice for using EVT. This method also generates the line numbering-feature in EVT:

<l n="26">Upon whose changeful face</l>

Line numbering caused some complications when creating the critical version of some poems which include full line deletions within the manuscript. **N.B. CIARA: YOU NEED TO COMPLETE THIS PARAGRAPH**

Manuscript features
Deletions and additions

Deletions and additions are a significant feature of interest when creating the edition. By examining the changes Brontë is making on the page I can illuminate and analyse the conscious development of her critical and poetic voice.

In the documentary edition, the recommended approach for a deletion is to use the <del> element which "contains a letter, word, or passage deleted, marked as deleted, or otherwise indicated as superfluous or spurious in the copy text by an author, scribe, or a previous annotator or corrector" [3.5.3]. Within EVT this element is visualised as a strikethrough which accurately fits Brontë's authorial habit of striking through deleted words.

For words added by Brontë above the main line of the text, <add> was used. This element "contains letters, words, or phrases inserted in the source text by an author, scribe, or a previous annotator or corrector" [3.5.3] and EVT displays the encoded text above the line.

Deletions and above-line additions often feature in pairs within the manuscripts, here is an example of the encoding within the documentary edition, where Brontë deleted the word "peace" and replaced it with the word "bliss" above the line:

<l n="170">And <del>peace</del>
    <add>bliss</add> shall rule for evermore</l>

A different approach was needed within the critical edition as the deletions and additions only apply to the MS, meaning that just <del> or <add> by itself could give the impression that the feature occurs in all versions of the text. Therefore, in the critical version I decided to nest these features within the critical apparatus encoding framework of <app> and <lem>, to make it clear that the changes only relate to one specific witness.

Here is an example of a deletion within the critical version:

<app>
    <lem wit="#MS-P">
        <del>songs</del>
    </lem>
</app>
Emphasis

At times Brontë underlines certain words within her manuscripts. In printed texts this usually corresponds to the capitalisation of the words.

In order to accurately convey this phenomenon within the manuscript, the most effective element is <hi> which "marks a word or phrase as graphically distinct from the surrounding text" [3.3.2.2].

This leads EVT to render the text as underlined, ensuring that the text is accurate and the reader can see the additional emphasis relating to the word in question. An example:

<hi rend="underline">will</hi>
Extra-texual manuscript information

Brontë often records the poem’s total number of lines somewhere within the manuscript, most often on either the first or final page. As it is often placed in isolation rather than as part of a particular pre-existing element of text, I use the anonymous block element, <ab>, in the relevant location to record these notes. This element "contains any component-level unit of text, acting as a container for phrase or inter level elements analogous to, but without the same constraints as, a paragraph" [17.3], making it flexible for this use-case. For example:

<ab>24 lines</ab>

Many of Anne Brontë's manuscripts include a signature and date after the poem. In order to record this information in the document while demarcating it from the body of the poem I opted for the <closer> element after the text of the poem. This element "groups together salutations, datelines, and similar phrases appearing as a final group at the end of a division, especially of a letter" [4.2.2].

The <closer> is contained within a <div> element so that EVT is able to correctly process the text of the closer as a discrete and valid portion of text.

Within the <closer> element, the signature is then indicated with a <signed> element while a date is included in the <dateline> element. For example:

<div>
    <closer>
        <signed>A B</signed>
        <dateline>August 19th 1841</dateline>
    </closer>
</div>
Witness variation

For this edition, I chose to document the variations between different versions of the texts produced by Brontë to get a sense of how later editions of the poem differ from the original manuscript. These changes often comprise spelling changes and punctuation, although substantive textual changes also occur and provide valuable insights into Brontë's literary, philosophical and aesthetic approach.

All variations between the witnesses are recorded in an <app> element. The <app> element "contains one entry in a critical apparatus, with an optional lemma and usually one or more readings or notes on the relevant passage" [13.1.1].

This edition is one that foregrounds the manuscript because part of the function of creating this digital edition is to provide direct access to Brontë's original versions of these poems for the first time by including images of original manuscripts. This gives the edition user the opportunity to encounter important manuscript features such as Brontë's original punctuation and crucial revisions in an immediate and unfiltered way.

The critical edition also includes the 1846 versions of the texts. This version presents the user with the evolution of the poem's texts, illuminating the textual history of the poems.

Therefore, it was appropriate to designate <lem> as referring to manuscripts as <lem> "contains the lemma, or base text, of a textual variation" [1.1]. Meanwhile, the variant readings from 1846 Poems are indicated by <rdg> which "contains a single reading within a textual variation" [12.1]

A typical <app> in which there are different readings between the MS and other witness is encoded as follows:

<l n="21">When <app>
        <lem wit="#MS-Q">friendly intercourse</lem>
        <rdg wit="#ACE">speech and mirth at once</rdg>
    </app>
</l>

Where a word or punctuation mark is not present in all versions of the text then only those witnesses which do include a feature in that textual position are included within the <app>.

For example:

<l n="28">… might trace<app>
        <rdg wit="#ACE">;</rdg>
    </app>
</l>
Editorial notes

The <note> element is the obvious choice for inline notes. The element "contains a note or annotation" [3.9.1] that renders as an interactable coloured dot similar to an asterisk within EVT, allowing extra information to be provided without unduly cluttering the reading view of the poems.

It has been used throughout the edition to highlight or expand on interesting features of the MS. The content comprises of concise comments on MS deletions or rewrites or relevant extra-textual info such as title changes.

For example:

<note>This poem is entitled "A Fragment" in both the MS and Chitham and was named
    "Self-Congratulation" in 1846 Poems.</note>

More significant editorial commentary has been included in individual editor's provided at the bottom of each poem. Placed after the text of the poem and the inclusion of closing info this info is nested within a new <div type="editor’s_note">.

There is then a <head> element to allow me to title the note in a way that is visible to the reader. Finally the text of the note itself is contained within <p> element which "marks paragraphs in prose" [3.1] These choices ensure that change in author and type of information here is made clear both for the internal working of the xml file and for the user of the edition.

Here is an example:

<div type="editor’s_note">
    <head>Editor's Note</head>
    <p> This 1838 poem is an early Gondal poem exploring themes of family, regret and nostalgia and
        was not published during Bronte's lifetime. (…)</p>
</div>

In terms of content, each note provides some background to the textual history of the poem, including information on the composition, manuscript location and whether they were published by Brontë herself or posthumously. I then provide concise critical summaries of each poem before highlighting and analysing the most striking features of the MS and/or revisions to the 1846 publications.
